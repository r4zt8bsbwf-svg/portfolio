AquaControl

An embedded aquarium automation platform — relay control, scheduling logic, and a manual override switchboard in one centralized controller.

Role Electrical design, firmware
Stack Microcontroller · Relays · Embedded C
Type Embedded systems
Status Deployed

The idea

Freshwater aquariums need a half-dozen subsystems running on schedules — lighting, filtration, heater, air pump, CO₂, feeding. AquaControl consolidates them under one embedded controller with both automatic schedules and a manual override switchboard for maintenance and emergencies.

The dual-mode design — automatic schedules with a physical override switchboard — turned out to be the most important decision in the whole project.

Two operating modes

Automatic

The controller runs lights, pumps, heaters, and timed subsystems on programmed schedules with conditional activation.

Manual override

A physical switchboard gives direct subsystem control — flipping any switch overrides the automation for that channel. Useful for emergency overrides, maintenance, or testing hardware without writing code.

Electrical

  • Relay-driven switching for AC-powered equipment
  • Discrete logic for the manual override interface
  • Embedded microcontroller as the central scheduler

The relay bank controls pumps, lighting circuits, heating elements, and aeration. Higher-current switching needed careful attention to noise, isolation, and contact ratings.

Firmware

  • Timed automation cycles per subsystem
  • Conditional activation based on schedule state
  • Safety logic to prevent simultaneous conflicting subsystems
  • User override handling that defers to physical switches

Hardware integrated

Pumps, heaters, lighting circuits, aeration systems, automated feeders, and the manual control switches. The design priorities throughout were reliability, electrical safety, ease of maintenance, and being able to expand later without re-architecting the firmware.

What it taught me

Relay noise will fight you the entire build — debouncing, snubbers, isolation, all of it. Safely switching higher-current AC devices from a low-voltage microcontroller is not just a wiring problem, it's a layout problem. And designing for manual override from day one — rather than bolting it on — is what made the system actually trustworthy around live animals.
